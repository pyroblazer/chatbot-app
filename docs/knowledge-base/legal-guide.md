## Contract Law

### Elements of a Valid Contract
For a contract to be legally enforceable, it generally requires:
1. **Offer**: A clear proposal by one party to do or refrain from doing something
2. **Acceptance**: Unequivocal agreement to the offer's exact terms (mirror image rule)
3. **Consideration**: Something of value exchanged by each party (money, services, promise)
4. **Capacity**: All parties must be of legal age and mental competency
5. **Legality**: The contract's purpose must be legal
6. **Mutual assent**: "Meeting of the minds" - both parties understand and agree to the same terms

### Common Contract Clauses to Understand
- **Indemnification**: One party agrees to compensate the other for specified losses or damages. Read carefully - broad indemnification can expose you to significant liability.
- **Limitation of liability**: Caps the amount one party can recover. Often favors the party with more negotiating power.
- **Arbitration clause**: Requires disputes to be resolved through arbitration rather than court. Often waives right to jury trial and class action.
- **Non-compete / Non-solicitation**: Restricts your ability to work for competitors or poach clients/employees. Enforceability varies widely by state.
- **NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)**: Protects confidential information. Key: what is confidential, duration, carve-outs (publicly available info, independent development).
- **Force majeure**: Excuses performance due to extraordinary events (natural disasters, pandemics). COVID highlighted importance of specific language.
- **Governing law / Jurisdiction**: Which state's law applies and where disputes are resolved.
- **Termination clauses**: How and when either party can end the contract, and consequences.

### Contract Red Flags
- Unilateral modification rights (one party can change terms without consent)
- Automatic renewal clauses with short cancellation windows
- Very broad indemnification with no cap
- One-sided dispute resolution in another jurisdiction
- Vague scope of work or payment terms

## Employment Law (US Overview)

### At-Will Employment
- Most US states are "at-will": either employer or employee can end employment at any time for any legal reason (or no reason), without notice.
- Exceptions: cannot fire for discriminatory reasons (protected classes), retaliation for protected activities (whistleblowing, filing complaints), or in violation of an employment contract.
- Montana is the only state with "wrongful discharge" law requiring just cause after a probationary period.

### Protected Classes (Federal Law)
Under Title VII, ADA, ADEA, and other federal laws, employers cannot discriminate based on:
- Race, color, national origin, religion, sex (Title VII)
- Age 40+ (Age Discrimination in Employment Act)
- Disability (Americans with Disabilities Act)
- Pregnancy (Pregnancy Discrimination Act)
- Genetic information (GINA)
Many states add additional protected classes (sexual orientation, gender identity, marital status, etc.)

### Wage and Hour Laws
- **Minimum wage**: Federal minimum is $7.25/hour. Many states and cities have higher minimums.
- **Overtime**: Non-exempt employees get 1.5x pay for hours over 40/week under FLSA.
- **Exempt vs Non-exempt**: Exempt employees (executive, professional, administrative roles meeting salary threshold) are not entitled to overtime.
- **Independent contractor vs employee**: Misclassification is a major legal issue. IRS uses multi-factor test. Contractors don't get benefits, overtime, or employer tax contributions.

### FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act)
- Applies to employers with 50+ employees.
- Eligible employees can take up to 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected leave per year for:
  - Birth/adoption/foster placement of child
  - Serious health condition of employee or immediate family member
  - Qualifying military exigencies
- Employee must have worked 12 months and 1,250 hours in past year.

### Non-Compete Agreements
- Enforceability varies dramatically by state. California, Minnesota, North Dakota ban them almost entirely.
- Courts look at: reasonableness of geographic scope, duration, legitimate business interest, harm to employee.
- FTC proposed rule (2024) to ban most non-competes nationally - check current status.

## Tenant Rights

### Lease Agreement Basics
- Read entire lease before signing. Key terms: rent amount/due date, security deposit, lease term, renewal terms, pet policy, subletting rights, maintenance responsibilities.
- Oral leases can be legally binding for month-to-month arrangements but are hard to enforce.
- Always get move-in/move-out checklist signed to protect your security deposit.

### Security Deposits
- Most states limit security deposits to 1-3 months' rent.
- Landlord must return deposit within state-mandated timeframe (14-30 days is common) after move-out.
- Landlord can deduct only for damage beyond normal wear and tear (not for cleaning standard apartment, painting walls white, normal carpet wear).
- If landlord wrongfully withholds deposit, many states allow you to sue for 2-3x the amount.

### Habitability Standards (Implied Warranty of Habitability)
Landlords must maintain:
- Working heat, plumbing, and electricity
- Weatherproofing (roof, walls, windows)
- Absence of serious health hazards (lead paint disclosure, mold, pests)
- Working smoke and CO detectors
- Common areas in safe condition

Tenant remedies for habitability violations: repair and deduct (in some states), rent withholding (with proper procedure), lease termination, or sue for damages.

### Eviction Process
Landlord cannot simply change locks or remove belongings - this is "self-help eviction" and illegal everywhere in the US.
Formal eviction process:
1. Written notice to cure or quit (3-30 days depending on violation and state)
2. If not cured: file unlawful detainer lawsuit in court
3. Court hearing (tenant has right to appear and defend)
4. If landlord wins: writ of possession issued
5. Sheriff or marshal executes eviction (not landlord)

Tenant defenses: improper notice, retaliatory eviction, failure to maintain habitability, acceptance of rent after notice.

## Intellectual Property

### Copyright
- Automatically created when original work is fixed in tangible form (writing, code, music, art, photos)
- No registration required, but registration gives significant benefits (ability to sue for statutory damages, attorney fees)
- Duration: Life of creator + 70 years (works created after 1978)
- **Fair Use**: Limited use of copyrighted material without permission. Factors: purpose (commercial vs educational), nature of work, amount used, market impact. Not a bright line - evaluated case by case.
- Work-for-hire: Works created by employees within scope of employment belong to employer.

### Trademark
- Protects brand names, logos, slogans used in commerce
- Rights come from USE, not registration (though federal registration with USPTO gives major benefits)
- Must actively enforce trademark or risk losing it (don't ignore infringement)
- Cannot trademark generic or purely descriptive terms
- Trademark search before launching a brand is strongly advised

### Patent
- Protects inventions and innovations for 20 years from filing date
- Must be novel, non-obvious, and useful
- Process is expensive ($10,000-$30,000+) and takes 2-5 years
- Must be filed before public disclosure in most countries (US has 1-year grace period)
- Provisional patent application: cheaper, establishes priority date, gives 12 months to file full application

### Trade Secrets
- Protected without registration, indefinitely, as long as kept secret
- Must take reasonable steps to maintain secrecy (NDAs, access controls, employee training)
- Protection lost if secret is independently discovered or reverse engineered
- Misappropriation of trade secrets is a federal crime under Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA)

## Consumer Protection

### FDCPA (Fair Debt Collection Practices Act)
Debt collectors (third-party, not original creditors) cannot:
- Call before 8am or after 9pm
- Call your workplace if told not to
- Use abusive, threatening, or deceptive language
- Discuss your debt with others (except spouse and attorney)
- Continue contact if you send written cease-and-desist

Your rights: request debt validation in writing within 30 days of first contact; dispute incorrect debts.

### FCRA (Fair Credit Reporting Act)
- You have right to free credit reports annually from each bureau (annualcreditreport.com)
- Dispute inaccurate information with credit bureaus - they must investigate within 30 days
- Negative items (late payments, collections) fall off after 7 years; bankruptcies after 10 years
- Employers, landlords must get written permission to pull credit report

## Business Law Basics

### Business Entities
- **Sole proprietorship**: Simplest, no formation needed. Owner is personally liable for all debts.
- **Partnership**: Default when 2+ people do business together. Partners are personally liable.
- **LLC (Limited Liability Company)**: Liability protection + flexible taxation. No corporate formalities. Default "pass-through" taxation (profits taxed on personal return). State formation documents required.
- **S-Corporation**: Tax election that allows pass-through taxation. Limits on number/type of shareholders. Can save on self-employment taxes when structured properly.
- **C-Corporation**: Standard corporation. Double taxation (corporate + dividend). Preferred for venture-funded startups (allows multiple share classes).

### LLC Best Practices
- Operating agreement: critical document governing ownership, management, profit distribution. Get one even if not required by state.
- Maintain separation: separate bank account, don't mix personal and business finances (preserves liability protection).
- Annual filings and fees vary by state.
- "Piercing the corporate veil" - courts can hold owners personally liable if entity formalities are ignored.
