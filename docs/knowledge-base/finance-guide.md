# Personal Finance Knowledge Base

## Budgeting Frameworks

### The 50/30/20 Rule
- **50% Needs**: Rent/mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments, transportation to work
- **30% Wants**: Dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, hobbies, non-essential shopping
- **20% Savings/Debt**: Emergency fund, retirement contributions, extra debt payments, investments
- Adjust percentages based on income level. High cost-of-living areas may need 60/20/20.

### Zero-Based Budgeting
- Every dollar of income is assigned a job (expenses + savings + investments = income).
- Forces intentional spending. Good for those who want maximum control.
- Tools: YNAB (You Need a Budget), EveryDollar.

### Tracking Methods
- Automatic tracking: Mint, Personal Capital, YNAB, Copilot (links bank accounts)
- Manual tracking: Spreadsheet or notebook - more awareness but more effort
- Weekly review: 10 minutes each week to review spending against budget

## Emergency Fund

### Why It Matters
- Prevents going into debt when unexpected expenses hit (car repair, medical bill, job loss)
- Reduces financial anxiety and decision-making under stress

### How Much
- General guideline: 3-6 months of essential living expenses
- Higher risk situations (single income, commission-based pay, unstable industry): 6-12 months
- Lower risk (dual income, stable job, low fixed expenses): 3 months may suffice

### Where to Keep It
- **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)**: Earns 4-5% APY (as of 2024), FDIC insured, fully liquid. Best option.
- Options: Marcus (Goldman Sachs), Ally Bank, SoFi, Discover Bank, Fidelity Cash Management
- NOT in: checking account (low yield), stock market (too volatile), CDs (limited liquidity)

## Debt Management

### Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)
- List all debts by interest rate, highest to lowest
- Make minimum payments on all debts
- Put all extra money toward the highest-interest debt first
- Saves the most money in interest over time

### Snowball Method (Psychologically Effective)
- List all debts by balance, smallest to largest
- Pay off smallest balances first regardless of interest rate
- Momentum and quick wins keep motivation high
- May cost more in interest but completion rate is higher

### Good Debt vs Bad Debt
- **Good debt**: Low interest, builds net worth or income (mortgage, student loans for high-ROI degrees, business loans)
- **Bad debt**: High interest, depreciating assets (credit card debt at 20%+, payday loans, car loans for depreciating vehicles)
- General rule: Aggressively pay off any debt above 6-7% interest before investing (beats most market returns net of risk)

### Credit Cards
- Pay balance in full every month to avoid 20%+ interest
- Use for rewards (1-5% cash back or travel points) only if you can pay it off
- Credit utilization: keep below 30% of credit limit for credit score health (ideally below 10%)
- Credit score factors: payment history (35%), utilization (30%), length of history (15%), credit mix (10%), new credit (10%)

## Investing Fundamentals

### Core Principles
- **Time in market beats timing the market**: Missing the 10 best days per decade dramatically reduces returns
- **Compound interest**: $10,000 at 8% for 30 years = $100,627. Start early.
- **Diversification**: Spread risk across asset classes, geographies, sectors
- **Low-cost index funds**: Outperform ~80-90% of actively managed funds over 10+ year periods (S&P 500 index)
- **Risk tolerance**: Match investments to time horizon and emotional capacity for loss
- **Dollar-cost averaging**: Invest fixed amount regularly regardless of market conditions. Removes emotion.

### Asset Classes
- **Stocks (equities)**: Ownership in companies. Higher return, higher volatility. Long-term (10+ years).
- **Bonds (fixed income)**: Loans to governments/corporations. Lower return, lower volatility. Stability and income.
- **Real estate**: REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts) allow real estate exposure without direct ownership.
- **Cash/cash equivalents**: HYSAs, money market funds. Preserve capital, low return.
- **International stocks**: Diversify beyond US market. Emerging and developed markets.

### Index Funds vs Active Funds
- **Index funds**: Track a market index (S&P 500, total market). Very low fees (0.03-0.20% expense ratio). Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab.
- **Active funds**: Fund manager picks stocks. Higher fees (0.5-1.5%+). Most underperform their benchmark long-term.
- **ETFs vs Mutual Funds**: ETFs trade like stocks (intraday), typically lower minimums. Mutual funds priced at end of day.

### Simple Portfolio Strategies
- **Three-fund portfolio**: Total US stock market + Total international stocks + US bonds. Adjust allocation by age.
- **Target-date funds**: All-in-one fund that automatically rebalances to more conservative allocation as you near retirement.
- Rule of thumb: Bond allocation = your age (e.g., 30 years old = 30% bonds, 70% stocks). Modern advice is more aggressive given longer lifespans.

## Retirement Accounts (US)

### 401(k) / 403(b)
- Employer-sponsored plan. Contributions are pre-tax (Traditional) or post-tax (Roth).
- 2024 contribution limit: $23,000 ($30,500 if age 50+)
- **Always contribute enough to get full employer match** - it's a 50-100% instant return on investment.
- Traditional 401(k): Tax deduction now, taxed on withdrawal in retirement.
- Roth 401(k): No tax deduction now, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

### IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
- 2024 contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)
- **Traditional IRA**: May be tax-deductible depending on income and employer plan coverage. Taxed on withdrawal.
- **Roth IRA**: No deduction. Tax-free growth and withdrawals. Income limits apply (~$161,000 single / ~$240,000 married in 2024 to contribute directly).
- Roth IRA is generally better for younger/lower-income earners (more years of tax-free growth).
- **Backdoor Roth IRA**: Strategy for high earners to contribute to Roth IRA via non-deductible Traditional IRA.

### HSA (Health Savings Account)
- Only available with High-Deductible Health Plans (HDHP)
- Triple tax advantage: contributions pre-tax, growth tax-free, withdrawals for medical expenses tax-free
- After age 65, can withdraw for any purpose (taxed like Traditional IRA)
- 2024 limits: $4,150 individual / $8,300 family
- Strategy: Pay medical expenses out of pocket now, invest HSA, withdraw tax-free in retirement for medical costs.

### Priority Order for Investing
1. 401(k) up to employer match (free money)
2. Pay off high-interest debt (>6-7%)
3. Max out HSA (if eligible)
4. Max out Roth IRA
5. Max out 401(k) remaining
6. Taxable brokerage account

## Tax Optimization

### Tax-Advantaged Accounts
- Use pre-tax accounts (Traditional 401k, IRA) if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.
- Use post-tax accounts (Roth) if you expect to be in a higher bracket in retirement.
- Diversify across both for tax flexibility in retirement (tax diversification).

### Capital Gains
- **Short-term capital gains**: Assets held less than 1 year. Taxed as ordinary income (up to 37%).
- **Long-term capital gains**: Assets held more than 1 year. Taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income.
- Strategy: Hold investments for at least 1 year before selling to qualify for lower rates.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting
- Sell investments at a loss to offset capital gains.
- Can deduct up to $3,000 of net capital losses against ordinary income per year.
- Carry forward excess losses to future years.
- Watch for wash-sale rule: cannot buy same or substantially identical security within 30 days before/after sale.

## Financial Independence (FIRE)

### The 4% Rule (Safe Withdrawal Rate)
- Based on Trinity Study: a portfolio of 50-75% stocks can sustain 4% annual withdrawals for 30 years.
- To retire, you need approximately 25x your annual expenses invested.
- Example: $60,000/year expenses -> need $1,500,000 invested.

### FIRE Variants
- **Lean FIRE**: Retire on very low expenses (~$25,000-$40,000/year). Requires frugal lifestyle.
- **Fat FIRE**: Retire with high expenses ($100,000+/year). Requires larger nest egg.
- **Barista FIRE / Coast FIRE**: Accumulate enough to coast to retirement with part-time work or stop contributing and let compound growth do the rest.

### Net Worth Milestones
- $1,000 emergency fund (before investing)
- Debt free except mortgage
- 3-6 months emergency fund
- 15% of income going to retirement
- House paid off
- Children's college funded
- Maximum retirement contributions
- Build wealth to give generously
